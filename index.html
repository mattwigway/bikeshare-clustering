Bay Area Bike Share Trip Clustering

These graphs represent a clustering of Bay Area Bike Share's trip data by accessibility ratios. Accessibility is a measure of how many things are within a certain distance of a place, and an accessibility ratio is the ratio of the accessibilities at the start and end of the trip. So an accessibility ratio of 7:1 for jobs within 10 minutes would mean that there are seven times more jobs within ten minutes walking distance of the start station than the end station.

Clustering assigns the trips to groups with similar accessibility ratios. The hypothesis is that there are several kinds of trips served by bikeshare; for example, commuter trips would have large differences in accessibility to jobs at the start and end stations (because they are try to access employment zones). For each cluster, I also computed the percentage of trips that were made by casual users (users without an annual membership) and the percentage that were made on the weekend, in order to evaluate the meanings of the cluster. These other variables were not used to perform the clustering, so any differences observed suggest differences in trip-making patterns between casual users and subscribers and between weekend and weekday trips.

I created four clusters of trips based on accessibility differences, as shown in the graphs above. Bars above the line indicate that the start station is more accessible in that category, and bars below the line indicate that the end station is more accessible. You will note that clusters 1 and 2, and 3 and 4, are nearly mirror images of each other; this makes sense as many people make round trips. For example, a trip from home to work would have a very low score for the jobs accessibility ratio (because jobs are much more accessible at the end), and the corresponding work-to-home trip would have the opposite.

Clusters 1 and 2 appear to represent commuters; there are large differences in the job accessibility between start and end stations, while other variables are less important. In clusters 3 and 4, jobs do not dominate and indeed the differences in accessibilty between the start and end of trips is not as large. Cluster 4 in particular has high levels of weekend and casual trip making, although cluster 3 also has higher levels than the commute clusters. This suggests that weekend and casual trips have different patterns than commute trips: they are less likely to be focused on entering or leaving an employment center.

This information can be used in bikeshare system planning. Knowing that different types of users make different types of trips can help predict, for example, trip patterns or rebalancing needs. Further research could include adding more types of accessibility (accessibility to transit stations would be useful, as many use bikeshare to access transit; this project used accessibility measures that had been computed previously and did not include accessibility to transit stations). It would also be interesting to cluster the users of a bikeshare system by their demographic characteristics to see if different groups of people have different trip-making patterns.
